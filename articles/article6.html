The Power of Linking Data: Primary and Secondary Keys

Here's where it gets exciting â€” and don't worry, this sounds more technical than it actually is. I'm talking about the idea of primary and secondary keys.

In simple terms, this just means finding what ties your documents together. What's the common thread that runs through multiple documents related to the same business activity?

Your truck registration might be the primary key. Registration number BFN123 appears on the delivery note, the diesel slip, the maintenance record, the driver assignment sheet. That registration number is the thread that connects all these documents to the same vehicle and the same operational activity.

Your trip ID, date, or ticket number might act as secondary keys. Trip number 2847 appears on the delivery note and the invoice. The date "2025-10-15" appears on the diesel slip, the delivery note, and the timesheet. These additional keys help you link documents even when they don't all contain the truck registration.

Once you start joining information by these keys â€” connecting, say, a diesel slip to the delivery note to the invoice, all related to the same trip â€” you unlock a story that's richer and truer than any single document could tell.

You stop seeing paperwork as admin, and start seeing relationships between activities, costs, and outcomes.

You see that Trip 2847 with Truck BFN123 consumed X liters of diesel, delivered Y tons over Z kilometers, generated R revenue, and produced a specific profit margin. Now you can compare that to Trip 2848 with Truck BFN456 and see which was more efficient. Which route performed better. Which truck is more fuel-efficient.

This is the shift from drowning in documents to swimming in insights.

Where to Start: A Simple Four-Step Process

Here's a simple way to begin unlocking the intelligence hidden in your paperwork. You can start this tomorrow with nothing more than your existing documents and a spreadsheet.

1. Collect

Start gathering your common business documents consistently. Don't try to go back and digitize five years of history. Start with today going forward.

Pick your most frequent document types: delivery notes, diesel slips, invoices, proof of deliveries. Every time one is created, capture it immediately. Take a photo, save the digital version, record it in a central location.

Make this a non-negotiable habit. Every document, every day, captured consistently.

2. Identify Keys

Find one or two fields that appear across multiple documents â€” these are your linking keys.

For most logistics and operations businesses, Truck ID or Registration Number is the obvious primary key. It appears on almost everything.

Date is another universal key. Almost every document is dated.

Trip ID or Ticket Number works if you use sequential numbering for jobs.

Customer Name helps link invoices to deliveries.

You don't need ten keys. Start with two or three that appear most consistently across your important documents.

3. Join the Dots

Combine the data in one place â€” even a simple spreadsheet. Create a structure where each row represents one trip or one transaction, and columns capture the key information from different documents.

Your columns might look like:

Date | Truck Reg | Trip ID | Customer | Route | Tons | Distance (km) | Diesel (L) | Diesel Cost | Revenue | Driver | Hours | Profit

When you enter data from a delivery note, you fill in Date, Truck, Customer, Route, Tons. When the diesel slip comes in for that same trip (matched by Truck and Date), you add Diesel and Diesel Cost. When the invoice is issued (matched by Trip ID or Customer and Date), you add Revenue.

Now your data is connected. Row by row, you're building a complete picture of each business activity with information from multiple documents.

4. Ask Questions

This is where it gets interesting. Once your data is connected, start interrogating it.

What's the relationship between distance, diesel consumption, and profit? Are longer trips more or less profitable per kilometer?

Which customer or route is most profitable? Not which generates the most revenue, but which delivers the best profit margin after all costs?

Which vehicle is most fuel-efficient? Are some trucks consuming 20% more diesel for similar routes? Why?

Which days or times see the longest delivery times? Is Friday always slower because of traffic? Can you schedule differently?

Which customers pay fastest versus slowest? Should you prioritize work from fast-paying customers when capacity is tight?

You'll be surprised how much your documents start "talking" once they're in one place and connected. Patterns emerge. Outliers become obvious. Questions you never thought to ask suddenly become answerable.

The Tools: Simpler Than You Think

You don't need expensive enterprise software to start doing this. The barrier to entry is lower than most people think.

Google Sheets is genuinely sufficient for most small and medium businesses. It's free, collaborative, accessible from anywhere, and has enough power to handle thousands of records and complex formulas.

Microsoft Excel works just as well if you prefer it.

For slightly more sophistication, Airtable gives you spreadsheet functionality with database-like relationships between tables, making it easier to link records across different document types.

If you want visual dashboards, connect your Google Sheet to Google Looker Studio for free visualizations, or use Excel with Power BI if you're in the Microsoft world.
